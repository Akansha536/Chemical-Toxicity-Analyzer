Glossary of Terms

LD50 (Lethal Dose 50%)

The dose at which 50% of test subjects die. Lower = more toxic. Measured in mg/kg body weight.

Toxicity

The degree to which a substance can damage an organism.

Bioaccumulation

When a chemical builds up in an organism faster than it can be eliminated.

Environmental Persistence

How long a chemical lasts in the environment before breaking down.

Carcinogen

A substance that can cause cancer in living tissue.

Neurotoxin

A substance that specifically damages the nervous system.

ppm (parts per million)

A unit of concentration. 1 ppm = 1 mg per liter of water.

GHS Classification

Globally Harmonized System for classifying chemicals by hazard type.

Real-World Case Studies

Minamata Disease (Japan, 1956)

Chemical: Methylmercury

Chisso Corporation dumped mercury into Minamata Bay. Over 2,000 people were poisoned, many with permanent neurological damage. This disaster led to strict environmental regulations in Japan.

Bhopal Disaster (India, 1984)

Chemical: Methyl isocyanate

Union Carbide plant leaked 42 tons of toxic gas, killing 16,000+ people immediately and causing 500,000 injuries. It remains the deadliest industrial disaster in history.

Flint Water Crisis (USA, 2014)

Chemical: Lead

Corrosive water leached lead from old pipes into drinking water. 100,000 residents exposed. Led to criminal charges and massive infrastructure overhaul.

Deepwater Horizon (USA, 2010)

Chemical: Crude Oil/Benzene

BP oil rig explosion killed 11 workers and spilled 134 million gallons of oil. Marine life devastated across the Gulf of Mexico ecosystem.

Love Canal (USA, 1978)

Chemical: Dioxin, Various Industrial Waste

87 homes built on former toxic waste dump. Families exposed to 200+ toxic chemicals. Evacuation of 900 people. Birth defect rates 3x higher than normal.

Seveso Disaster (Italy, 1976)

Chemical: TCDD (Dioxin)

Chemical plant explosion released dioxin cloud over 18,000 residents. Animals died, children suffered chloracne. Led to EU Seveso Directive for industrial safety.
